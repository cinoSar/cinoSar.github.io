1.1 — Frequentist vs. Bayesian Interpretations

Before diving into the mathematics, we must ask a foundational question: what does probability mean? Two schools of thought offer different answers.

The Frequentist View

In the frequentist interpretation, probability is the long-run relative frequency of an event. If we flip a fair coin infinitely many times, heads comes up 50% of the time. If unemployment rises 60% of the time when monetary policy tightens (in similar historical contexts), then we might assign probability 0.60 to unemployment rising given a policy tightening. Probability is about what happens repeatedly under similar conditions.

This view works well for repeatable experiments but struggles with one-off events. What is the probability that GDP growth exceeds 3% next year? Next year will happen only once, so the frequentist must appeal to a hypothetical long-run frequency — across many parallel universes, perhaps, or across many similar economic scenarios.

The Bayesian View

Bayesians interpret probability as degree of belief, updated by evidence. Before seeing the data, I might believe (prior) that a new policy has a 40% chance of success. After observing six months of data, I update to 70%. Probability is subjective, but it follows strict rules. This flexibility is powerful for one-off decisions — "What is the probability that Italian GDP growth exceeds 3% next year?" becomes a coherent question.

Which Do We Use Here?

This course is fundamentally frequentist. We fit linear regressions, construct confidence intervals, and run hypothesis tests — all frequentist machinery. Bayesian methods are powerful and increasingly used in applied work, but they require a different conceptual framework and more computational machinery. We'll touch on Bayes' theorem because it's fundamental, but most of what follows is frequentist.

Why this matters for econometrics

The frequentist-Bayesian divide shapes every decision you make: confidence intervals (frequentist) versus credible intervals (Bayesian), hypothesis tests (frequentist) versus posterior probabilities (Bayesian), OLS regression (neutral, but frequentist inference) versus Bayesian hierarchical models. Understanding both perspectives makes you a better applied researcher.

1.2 — Axioms: The Foundation of Probability

All of probability rests on three axioms, formalized by Andrey Kolmogorov. These axioms don't prove anything — they're the starting point. Every result that follows flows from them.

The Three Axioms

Axiom 1 (Non-negativity): Probability is never negative. $$\P(A) \geq 0 \text{ for all events } A$$

Axiom 2 (Unitarity): The probability of the entire sample space (all possible outcomes) is 1. $$\P(\Omega) = 1$$ where $\Omega$ is the sample space.

Axiom 3 (Additivity): If events are mutually exclusive (disjoint), the probability of their union is the sum of their probabilities. $$\P(A \cup B) = \P(A) + \P(B) \quad \text{if } A \cap B = \emptyset$$

From these three axioms, everything follows: conditional probability, independence, Bayes' theorem, laws of large numbers, central limit theorem. They are minimalist and elegant.

Why axioms matter

Axioms define the rules of the game. They ensure consistency: you can't assign probability 0.3 to event A, probability 0.6 to event B, and probability 0.8 to A or B if A and B are mutually exclusive (that would violate axiom 3). When you feel confused about probability, return to the axioms. They always cut through the fog.

1.3 — Conditional Probability: Updating on Evidence

Probability is rarely about the world in isolation. Almost always, we ask: given what we know, what is the probability of the event? This is conditional probability.

Definition and Intuition

The probability of event A given event B is denoted $\P(A \mid B)$ and defined as:

$$\P(A \mid B) = \frac{\P(A \cap B)}{\P(B)}, \quad \text{provided } \P(B) > 0$$

Intuition: of all the times B occurs, what fraction of those also have A? The numerator $\P(A \cap B)$ is the probability that both occur. The denominator $\P(B)$ is the probability of B alone. The ratio restricts our attention to the world where B is true.

A Concrete Example: Italian Workers

Suppose we have data on Italian workers. Among a large sample:

  • 30% hold a university degree
  • 20% earn more than €50,000 per year
  • 15% have both a degree and earn >€50,000

What is the probability that a worker earns >€50,000 given that they hold a degree?

Let $D$ = has degree, $H$ = earns >€50k. We know:

  • $\P(D) = 0.30$
  • $\P(H) = 0.20$
  • $\P(D \cap H) = 0.15$

Then:

$$\P(H \mid D) = \frac{\P(D \cap H)}{\P(D)} = \frac{0.15}{0.30} = 0.50$$

Among degree-holders, 50% earn over €50k. That's higher than the 20% population baseline, suggesting that a degree is associated with higher earnings.

Computing in Python

import pandas as pd import numpy as np # Simulate Italian workers data np.random.seed(42) n = 10000 # Create variables: degree (0/1) and high_earn (0/1) degree = np.random.binomial(n=1, p=0.30, size=n) # Earnings depend on degree: base prob 0.15, higher if degree p_earn = 0.15 + degree * 0.35 high_earn = np.random.binomial(n=1, p=p_earn, size=n) df = pd.DataFrame({"degree": degree, "high_earn": high_earn}) # Marginal probabilities p_degree = df["degree"].mean() p_high = df["high_earn"].mean() # Joint probability p_both = (df["degree"] == 1) & (df["high_earn"] == 1) p_both = p_both.mean() # Conditional probability P(high_earn | degree) p_high_given_degree = p_both / p_degree print(f"P(degree) = {p_degree:.3f}") print(f"P(high_earn) = {p_high:.3f}") print(f"P(degree AND high_earn) = {p_both:.3f}") print(f"P(high_earn | degree) = {p_high_given_degree:.3f}")
P(degree) = 0.300 P(high_earn) = 0.255 P(degree AND high_earn) = 0.152 P(high_earn | degree) = 0.507

The conditional probability is 0.507: among degree-holders, about 50.7% earn over €50k. This is far higher than the unconditional probability of 0.255.

Conditional probability in regression

Regression is fundamentally about conditional expectations. When we fit $\mathbb{E}[Y \mid X]$, we are asking: given the values of X, what do we expect Y to be on average? Conditional probability is the same idea for discrete outcomes.

1.4 — Bayes' Theorem: The Gold Standard

Bayes' theorem is perhaps the most important formula in statistics. It tells us how to update our beliefs in light of new evidence.

The Derivation

Start with the definition of conditional probability applied twice:

$$\P(A \mid B) = \frac{\P(A \cap B)}{\P(B)}$$

We can also write:

$$\P(B \mid A) = \frac{\P(A \cap B)}{\P(A)}$$

Rearranging the second equation:

$$\P(A \cap B) = \P(B \mid A) \cdot \P(A)$$

Substitute into the first equation:

$$\P(A \mid B) = \frac{\P(B \mid A) \cdot \P(A)}{\P(B)}$$

This is Bayes' theorem. In its more general form, if we have competing hypotheses $H_1, H_2, \ldots$:

$$\P(H_i \mid \text{Data}) = \frac{\P(\text{Data} \mid H_i) \cdot \P(H_i)}{\P(\text{Data})}$$

where

$$\P(\text{Data}) = \sum_j \P(\text{Data} \mid H_j) \cdot \P(H_j)$$

Terminology

  • Prior $\P(H_i)$: What we believed about the hypothesis before seeing data.
  • Likelihood $\P(\text{Data} \mid H_i)$: The probability of observing the data under hypothesis $H_i$.
  • Posterior $\P(H_i \mid \text{Data})$: What we believe after seeing data.
  • Marginal likelihood $\P(\text{Data})$: The average likelihood across all hypotheses.

Why Bayes' theorem matters

It formalizes learning. You start with a prior belief, observe data, and compute a posterior that incorporates both. This is how scientists actually think: hypotheses are revised in light of evidence. Frequentist methods focus on the likelihood and ignore the prior, treating hypotheses symmetrically. Bayesian methods embrace both.

1.5 — Independence: When Events Don't Influence Each Other

Two events are independent if knowing the outcome of one tells you nothing about the other.

Definition

Events A and B are independent if:

$$\P(A \cap B) = \P(A) \cdot \P(B)$$

Equivalently, $\P(A \mid B) = \P(A)$ and $\P(B \mid A) = \P(B)$. Conditioning on B doesn't change the probability of A.

The Italian Workers Example Revisited

In our earlier example: $\P(D) = 0.30$, $\P(H) = 0.20$, $\P(D \cap H) = 0.15$.

If degree and earnings were independent, we would expect:

$$\P(D \cap H) = 0.30 \times 0.20 = 0.06$$

But we observed $\P(D \cap H) = 0.15$, which is much higher. Degree and high earnings are strongly associated — they are not independent.

This association is the entire point of regression! When we fit a model like:

$$\text{earnings} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \text{degree} + \epsilon$$

we are quantifying the dependence between variables. A non-zero $\beta_1$ means degree and earnings are not independent.

A Caution: Dependence Is Not Causation

Just because D and H are dependent doesn't mean D causes H. Sector could be a confounder: high-paying sectors might require degrees. Conditional on sector, the dependence might disappear. This is the central problem of causal inference in observational data.

Confounding and statistical dependence

If a confounder C affects both D and H, then D and H will appear dependent even if D doesn't cause H. The fix: condition on C. We'll revisit this idea when we discuss confounders explicitly.

1.6 — Confounders and Mediators: Two Types of Third Variables

In the Italian workers example, we found that degree and earnings are dependent. But is the relationship causal? Not necessarily. A third variable could be driving both. Understanding the direction of causality (even conceptually) is crucial.

Confounders

A confounder affects both the treatment and the outcome. In our example, suppose sector is a confounder:

  • High-paying sectors (finance, tech) hire more degree-holders.
  • High-paying sectors also pay more, regardless of degree.

If we ignore sector and just compare earnings of degree-holders vs. non-degree-holders, we confound the effect of the degree with the effect of sector. The solution: stratify by sector or include sector as a control variable.

# Simulating with sector as a confounder np.random.seed(42) n = 10000 # Sector affects both degree-holding and earnings sector = np.random.binomial(n=1, p=0.4, size=n) # 1 = high-paying # Probability of degree depends on sector p_degree = 0.2 + sector * 0.3 degree = np.random.binomial(n=1, p=p_degree, size=n) # Earnings depend on sector, NOT on degree (after controlling for sector) base_earn = 30000 + sector * 40000 earnings = base_earn + np.random.normal(0, 5000, n) df = pd.DataFrame({"degree": degree, "earnings": earnings, "sector": sector}) # Naive comparison (confounded) naive_diff = df[df["degree"] == 1]["earnings"].mean() - df[df["degree"] == 0]["earnings"].mean() # Stratified by sector (unconfounded) diff_high_sector = df[(df["sector"] == 1)]["earnings"].mean() - df[(df["sector"] == 0)]["earnings"].mean() print(f"Naive effect of degree: €{naive_diff:.0f}") print(f"True effect of degree (by sector): €{diff_high_sector:.0f}")
Naive effect of degree: €18234 True effect of degree (by sector): €39765

The naive comparison suggests degrees add €18k, but that's confounded by sector. Stratifying by sector reveals that the real effect is sector choice, not the degree itself.

Mediators

A mediator lies on the causal pathway from treatment to outcome. Suppose degree causes sector choice, and sector choice causes earnings:

degree → sector → earnings

Sector is a mediator. If we control for sector, we "block" the causal pathway and estimate only the direct effect of degree on earnings. If we don't control for sector, we estimate the total effect (direct + indirect through sector).

Whether to control for a mediator depends on your research question. If you want the total effect of degree on earnings, don't control for sector. If you want to isolate the direct effect, do.

Confounders vs. Mediators: A Key Distinction

Confounders bias estimates — you must control for them. Mediators are on the causal path — whether to control depends on your goal. If you control for a confounder, bias decreases. If you control for a mediator, you remove the indirect effect and isolate the direct effect.

1.7 — Continuous Random Variables: Density and CDF

For discrete events (degree: yes/no, sector: high/low), probability mass functions work fine. But most econometric variables are continuous: income, GDP growth, temperature. The mathematics changes subtly but importantly.

Probability Density Function (PDF)

For a continuous random variable $X$, the probability density function $f(x)$ describes the relative likelihood of different values. Unlike probability mass functions, $f(x)$ is not itself a probability — it's a density.

The probability that $X$ falls in an interval is the area under the curve:

$$\P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_a^b f(x) \, dx$$

Crucially, $\P(X = x) = 0$ for any specific value $x$. This seems weird, but it's correct: there are uncountably many real numbers in any interval, so the probability of hitting exactly one is zero. This is why we always talk about ranges for continuous variables.

Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF)

The cumulative distribution function $F(x) = \P(X \leq x)$ is the probability that $X$ is at most $x$. It accumulates probability from $-\infty$ to $x$:

$$F(x) = \int_{-\infty}^x f(u) \, du$$

And therefore:

$$\P(a \leq X \leq b) = F(b) - F(a)$$

Why the PDF Can Exceed 1

A common source of confusion: if $f(x)$ is a probability, how can it exceed 1? The answer is that $f(x)$ is a density, not a probability. Consider the uniform distribution on [0, 0.1]:

$$f(x) = 10 \text{ for } x \in [0, 0.1]$$

The density is 10, which exceeds 1. But the probability is the area: $\int_0^{0.1} 10 \, dx = 1$. The density can be any non-negative number, as long as the total area under the curve is 1.

import scipy.stats as stats import matplotlib.pyplot as plt # Uniform distribution on [0, 0.1] x = np.linspace(-0.05, 0.15, 1000) dist = stats.uniform(loc=0, scale=0.1) pdf = dist.pdf(x) cdf = dist.cdf(x) # Probability in [0.02, 0.08] prob = dist.cdf(0.08) - dist.cdf(0.02) print(f"PDF at x=0.05: {dist.pdf(0.05):.1f}") print(f"P(0.02 ≤ X ≤ 0.08) = {prob:.2f}") print(f"Total area under PDF: {np.trapz(pdf, x):.2f}")
PDF at x=0.05: 10.0 P(0.02 ≤ X ≤ 0.08) = 0.60 Total area under PDF: 1.00

The density reaches 10 (exceeding 1), but the total area is exactly 1. The probability of landing in [0.02, 0.08] is 0.60 — the area of that region.

Normal Distribution: The Workhorse

The normal distribution, parameterized by mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$, is ubiquitous in econometrics. Its PDF is:

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

You don't need to memorize this. What matters: about 68% of data falls within $\mu \pm \sigma$, 95% within $\mu \pm 2\sigma$. Most regression relies on the assumption that errors are normally distributed. And the Central Limit Theorem (which we'll see later) ensures that sample means are approximately normal, even if the underlying data isn't.

Density vs. Probability: The Intuition

Think of density like a histogram with infinitesimal bins. The height of a bar is "density per unit width". To get probability, you multiply density by width (integrate). For a point, the width is zero, so probability is zero. This is why continuous distributions are defined by their CDF (which is a probability) rather than a PDF.

Key Takeaways

Probability is fundamental. Frequentists interpret it as long-run frequency; Bayesians as updated beliefs. This course is frequentist. Conditional probability $\P(A \mid B)$ is at the heart of regression. Bayes' theorem formalizes learning from data. Independence $\P(A \cap B) = \P(A) \cdot \P(B)$ is the null hypothesis of association. Confounders must be controlled; mediators are optional. Continuous variables have zero point probability and require integration to compute probabilities — use the CDF.
