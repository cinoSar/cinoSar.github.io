0.3 — Measures of Central Tendency

You already know what mean, median, and mode are. The interesting question is: when does each one tell you something useful, and when does it mislead you?

The Mean–Median Gap as a Diagnostic Tool

When mean ≈ median, the distribution is roughly symmetric. When they diverge, something interesting is happening — usually skewness. For a researcher, that divergence is a signal, not just a number.

summary = pd.DataFrame({ "mean": df[["gdp_per_capita", "life_expectancy", "gini_index"]].mean(), "median": df[["gdp_per_capita", "life_expectancy", "gini_index"]].median(), }) summary["gap_pct"] = (summary["mean"] - summary["median"]) / summary["median"] * 100
mean median gap_pct gdp_per_capita 11227.47 4403.52 155.0% life_expectancy 62.57 62.10 0.8% gini_index 34.47 32.30 6.7%

GDP per capita has a mean 155% higher than its median — a massive right skew. Life expectancy is almost perfectly symmetric. This pattern is typical of income-related variables and has direct consequences for regression.

Why this matters in econometrics

When the mean is substantially higher than the median, the distribution is right-skewed. This has three practical consequences: (1) OLS regression assumes normally distributed errors — skewed dependent variables can violate this; (2) the standard fix is log(GDP per capita) — the log transformation pulls in the right tail; (3) interpretation changes — coefficients in a log-linear model represent percentage changes, not absolute ones.

0.4 — Measures of Variability

Variability is at least as important as central tendency. Two datasets can have the same mean but tell completely different stories.

Standard Deviation: What It Actually Means

SD is the typical distance of observations from the mean. Not the maximum distance, not a boundary — a typical distance. For a normal distribution, about 68% of data falls within ±1 SD of the mean.

Coefficient of Variation: Comparing Apples and Oranges

SD is in the same units as your variable, which makes it useless for comparing variability across variables with different scales. GDP is in dollars; life expectancy in years. The Coefficient of Variation (CV) — SD divided by the mean, expressed as a percentage — solves this.

std IQR CV (%) gdp_per_capita 22591.14 6643.57 201.2 life_expectancy 11.23 14.00 17.9 gini_index 11.57 15.60 33.6

GDP per capita varies the most in relative terms (CV = 201%) — income inequality across countries is enormous. Life expectancy, despite having a similar absolute SD to the Gini index, varies much less relative to its mean.

When SD is misleading: enter the IQR

Just as the mean is sensitive to outliers, so is the standard deviation. The Interquartile Range (IQR) — the range covering the middle 50% of data — is a robust alternative. When SD and IQR tell different stories, you likely have outliers or heavy tails.

0.5 — The Shape of Distributions: Skewness and Kurtosis

Beyond centre and spread, distributions have shape. Two key descriptors:

Skewness measures asymmetry. Positive skewness means the right tail is longer (income, house prices). Negative skewness means the left tail is longer (exam scores near the maximum).

Kurtosis measures the heaviness of tails. High kurtosis means more extreme values than a normal distribution would predict. In finance and econometrics, this is crucial: financial returns have fat tails, which is why models assuming normality underestimate risk.

shape_stats = pd.DataFrame({ "skewness": df[["gdp_per_capita", "life_expectancy", "gini_index"]].skew(), "kurtosis": df[["gdp_per_capita", "life_expectancy", "gini_index"]].kurtosis() })
skewness kurtosis gdp_per_capita 5.54 40.09 life_expectancy 0.25 -0.55 gini_index 0.75 -0.09

GDP per capita has extreme skewness (5.54) and kurtosis (40.09) — this distribution is far from normal. Life expectancy is nearly symmetric (skewness ≈ 0) with slightly lighter tails than normal (negative kurtosis). Rules of thumb: skewness near 0 is symmetric; kurtosis near 0 is normal-like (pandas reports excess kurtosis).

0.6 — Relationships Between Variables: Correlation

In econometrics, we are almost always interested in relationships: does X affect Y? Before running regressions, we explore relationships visually and with correlation.

Pearson Correlation

Pearson’s r measures linear association, ranging from −1 to +1. A value near 0 means no linear relationship — but there could still be a strong non-linear one.

Key correlations: GDP ↔ Life expectancy: r = 0.62 GDP ↔ Gini: r = −0.26 Population: weakly correlated with everything

Scatter Plots: Always Visualise Before Correlating

A correlation coefficient is a single number that can hide patterns. In our dataset, GDP and life expectancy have r = 0.62 on the raw scale, but r = 0.93 after log-transforming GDP. The relationship is actually much stronger than the linear correlation suggests — it's log-linear.

Why log(GDP) is standard in econometrics

The log transformation serves three purposes: it linearises relationships that are curved on the raw scale, it reduces skewness, and it changes interpretation — a one-unit change in log(GDP) represents a percentage change in GDP. This is why you'll see log(income), log(price), and log(GDP) in virtually every empirical paper.

0.7 — Group Comparisons

In research, we rarely look at data as a monolith. We compare groups: countries by region, firms by sector, patients by treatment. The pandas groupby() method is the workhorse here.

regional = df.groupby("region")[["gdp_per_capita", "life_expectancy"]].agg( ["count", "mean", "median", "std"] )

Violin plots show the full distribution within each group, not just summary statistics — always prefer them over bar charts with error bars.

Key Takeaways

Always inspect your data first with .shape, .dtypes, .describe(), .head(). The mean–median gap is a diagnostic for skewness. Log transformations tame right-skewed variables and are standard in econometrics. The Coefficient of Variation lets you compare variability across scales. Visualise before computing — a correlation coefficient hides patterns that scatter plots reveal. And always remember: correlation ≠ causation.
