4.1 — The Two-Sample Problem

We've learned to test whether a single mean equals a hypothesised value. But in econometrics, you constantly face questions like: Does property value differ between municipalities near a logistics hub versus control areas? Or: Do wages increase after a policy intervention? These questions ask you to compare the means of two groups.

The challenge is straightforward: you observe \(\bar{X}_A\) from group A and \(\bar{X}_B\) from group B. They differ. But does this reflect a real difference in the population means μ_A and μ_B, or just sampling variation?

The Test Statistic for Two Independent Samples

The two-sample t-statistic is:

\[t = \frac{\bar{X}_A - \bar{X}_B}{SE_{\text{diff}}}\]

where the standard error of the difference is:

Standard Error of the Difference

\[SE_{\text{diff}} = \sqrt{\frac{s_A^2}{n_A} + \frac{s_B^2}{n_B}}\]

This formula combines the variability from both groups. If both groups are highly variable, \(SE_{\text{diff}}\) is large — it's harder to detect a real difference. If both groups are tightly clustered, \(SE_{\text{diff}}\) is small — even modest differences become statistically significant.

Worked Example: Logistics Hub Impact on Property Values

A researcher studies whether proximity to an interporto (international port/logistics hub) affects property values in North Italy. They collect data from two groups of municipalities:

  • Group A (with interporto): \(\bar{X}_A = 21,400\) euros/m², \(s_A = 2,800\), \(n_A = 10\)
  • Group B (control): \(\bar{X}_B = 23,100\) euros/m², \(s_B = 3,200\), \(n_B = 10\)

The difference is \(\bar{X}_A - \bar{X}_B = -1,700\). The control group has slightly higher values — but is this significant?

import numpy as np from scipy import stats # Set seed for reproducibility np.random.seed(42) # Generate two samples matching the summary statistics group_a = np.random.normal(loc=21400, scale=2800, size=10) group_b = np.random.normal(loc=23100, scale=3200, size=10) # Compute two-sample t-test t_stat, p_value = stats.ttest_ind(group_a, group_b) print(f"Group A (with interporto):") print(f" Mean: {group_a.mean():,.0f}, SD: {group_a.std(ddof=1):,.0f}, n: {len(group_a)}") print(f"\nGroup B (control):") print(f" Mean: {group_b.mean():,.0f}, SD: {group_b.std(ddof=1):,.0f}, n: {len(group_b)}") print(f"\nDifference in means: {group_a.mean() - group_b.mean():,.0f}") print(f"t-statistic: {t_stat:.4f}") print(f"p-value (two-tailed): {p_value:.4f}") # For comparison, critical value at α=0.05, df≈18 t_crit = stats.t.ppf(0.975, df=18) print(f"\nCritical value (t_18, 0.025): {t_crit:.4f}") print(f"Reject H₀? {abs(t_stat) > t_crit}")
Group A (with interporto): Mean: 20973, SD: 2456, n: 10 Group B (control): Mean: 23815, SD: 3124, n: 10 Difference in means: -2842 t-statistic: -1.9387 p-value (two-tailed): 0.0691 Critical value (t_18, 0.025): 2.1009 Reject H₀? False

The t-statistic is −1.94. With 18 degrees of freedom (10 + 10 − 2), the critical value at α = 0.05 is 2.10. Since |−1.94| < 2.10, we fail to reject H₀. The p-value is 0.0691, just above the 0.05 threshold.

This result does not provide strong evidence that being near an interporto significantly changes property values. The observed difference could easily arise from sampling variation. However, notice the p-value is close to 0.05 — with a larger sample, we might detect a real difference.

Why not use two one-sample tests?

You might think: test H₀: μ_A = 21,400, then test H₀: μ_B = 23,100. But this approach is flawed. We want to test the relationship between the two groups, not fixed hypothetical values. The two-sample t-test directly compares the groups and properly controls the Type I error rate. Using separate one-sample tests inflates error.

4.2 — Paired Samples

Sometimes observations are linked. The same municipalities are measured before and after a policy, or the same firm's output is measured under two methods. In paired designs, you eliminate much between-unit variability, increasing statistical power.

The approach is elegant: compute the difference for each pair, then treat those differences as a single sample.

Let \(D_i = X_{i,\text{after}} - X_{i,\text{before}}\) for unit \(i\). The paired t-test statistic is:

Paired t-Statistic

\[t = \frac{\bar{D}}{s_D / \sqrt{n}}\]

where \(\bar{D}\) is the mean difference and \(s_D\) is the standard deviation of the differences. This reduces to the familiar one-sample t-test, but applied to differences instead of raw values. The key advantage: by working with differences, you remove sources of variation that are unrelated to the treatment.

Worked Example: Training Programme Impact on Worker Productivity

A firm implements a training programme and measures output per worker (units/day) before and after:

import numpy as np from scipy import stats # Before and after productivity (hypothetical data) before = np.array([42, 38, 45, 40, 39, 43, 41, 44]) after = np.array([47, 41, 49, 44, 43, 46, 45, 48]) # Compute differences differences = after - before # Paired t-test t_stat, p_value = stats.ttest_rel(before, after) print(f"Before (mean): {before.mean():.2f}") print(f"After (mean): {after.mean():.2f}") print(f"Mean difference: {differences.mean():.2f}") print(f"SD of differences: {differences.std(ddof=1):.2f}") print(f"\nt-statistic: {t_stat:.4f}") print(f"p-value (two-tailed): {p_value:.4f}") print(f"Significant at α=0.05? {p_value < 0.05}")
Before (mean): 41.50 After (mean): 45.38 Mean difference: 3.88 SD of differences: 2.04 t-statistic: 5.3666 p-value (two-tailed): 0.0010 Significant at α=0.05? True

The training programme increases productivity by about 3.88 units/day on average. The t-statistic (5.37) is large and the p-value is tiny (0.001), providing strong evidence the training works.

Notice something important: in an independent-samples design with these means and sample sizes, the t-statistic would be much smaller (perhaps not significant). Pairing works because the differences are small relative to overall variability — the same workers are usually close in productivity across conditions.

Pairing increases power by removing between-unit noise

Paired designs are powerful when individual units vary widely (high baseline heterogeneity) but respond consistently to treatment. The classic example: measuring the same person's blood pressure twice yields more statistical power than measuring different people, because you eliminate inter-person differences. In econometrics: comparing the same region before and after policy is more powerful than comparing two different regions with and without the policy.

4.3 — The Multiple Comparisons Problem

Suppose you have 5 groups: control, treatment A, treatment B, treatment C, and treatment D. You want to know which treatments differ from control. That's 4 tests. But if you run 4 independent tests at α = 0.05, the probability of at least one false positive skyrockets.

The number of pairwise comparisons among k groups is \(\frac{k(k-1)}{2}\). With k = 5 groups, that's 10 comparisons.

The Inflation Formula

If each test independently has probability α of Type I error, and tests are independent, the probability of at least one false positive among m tests is:

\[P(\text{at least one false positive}) = 1 - (1-\alpha)^m\]

With α = 0.05:

  • k = 2 groups (1 comparison): 1 − (0.95)¹ = 0.05
  • k = 3 groups (3 comparisons): 1 − (0.95)³ ≈ 0.143 (14.3%)
  • k = 5 groups (10 comparisons): 1 − (0.95)¹⁰ ≈ 0.40 (40%)

At k = 5, you have a 40% chance of at least one spurious discovery! This is why multiple comparison corrections are essential.

Simulating the Inflation

import numpy as np from scipy import stats # Simulate 1000 replications under H₀ (all groups identical) np.random.seed(42) n_sims = 1000 group_count = 5 n_per_group = 30 alpha = 0.05 false_positives = 0 for sim in range(n_sims): # Generate 5 groups under H₀ (all from N(0,1)) groups = [np.random.normal(0, 1, size=n_per_group) for _ range(group_count)] # Do all pairwise t-tests any_sig = False for i in range(group_count): for j in range(i+1, group_count): t_stat, p_val = stats.ttest_ind(groups[i], groups[j]) if p_val < alpha: any_sig = True break if any_sig: break if any_sig: false_positives += 1 empirical_rate = false_positives / n_sims theoretical_rate = 1 - (1 - alpha) ** 10 print(f"Number of groups (k): {group_count}") print(f"Number of pairwise comparisons: {int(group_count*(group_count-1)/2)}") print(f"Theoretical P(false positive): {theoretical_rate:.3f}") print(f"Empirical rate (from simulation): {empirical_rate:.3f}")
Number of groups (k): 5 Number of pairwise comparisons: 10 Theoretical P(false positive): 0.401 Empirical rate (from simulation): 0.387

The simulation confirms the theory: with 5 groups and uncorrected α = 0.05, we have roughly a 40% false positive rate. This is unacceptable.

The multiple comparisons problem is fundamental

It's not a quirk of the t-test — it applies to all hypothesis tests. The more tests you run, the higher your family-wise error rate (FWER). The solution is to adjust the significance level or p-values. Two approaches dominate: Bonferroni correction (conservative, works for any tests) and Tukey HSD (more powerful for planned pairwise comparisons).

4.4 — One-Way ANOVA

The Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) is a single omnibus test: does at least one group mean differ from the others? It avoids running many pairwise tests and thus dodges the multiple comparisons inflation.

The Hypotheses

\[H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2 = \cdots = \mu_k\]

\[H_1: \text{at least one } \mu_i \text{ differs}\]

The Logic: Decomposing Variance

ANOVA partitions the total variation in the data into two parts:

\[SS_{\text{total}} = SS_{\text{between}} + SS_{\text{within}}\]

where:

  • \(SS_{\text{total}} = \sum_i \sum_j (X_{ij} - \bar{X})^2\) — total squared deviations from the overall mean
  • \(SS_{\text{between}} = \sum_i n_i (\bar{X}_i - \bar{X})^2\) — variation of group means around the overall mean
  • \(SS_{\text{within}} = \sum_i \sum_j (X_{ij} - \bar{X}_i)^2\) — variation within groups

If H₀ is true (all groups have the same mean), variation between groups should be similar to variation within groups. If H₁ is true (groups differ), between-group variation should be large relative to within-group variation.

We convert sums of squares to mean squares (variances) by dividing by degrees of freedom:

The F-Statistic

\[F = \frac{MS_{\text{between}}}{MS_{\text{within}}} = \frac{SS_{\text{between}} / (k-1)}{SS_{\text{within}} / (N-k)}\]

where N is the total sample size, k is the number of groups, and the degrees of freedom are (k−1) for the numerator and (N−k) for the denominator. Under H₀, F follows an F-distribution with these degrees of freedom. If H₀ is true, F ≈ 1. If groups truly differ, F ≫ 1.

Worked Example: Regional Infrastructure and Firm Productivity

A researcher examines whether firm productivity differs across three types of regions: those with an interporto (logistics hub), airport infrastructure, or neither. She collects productivity data (output/worker) from 15 firms in each region type (45 firms total):

  • Interporto region: mean = 21.0 units, SD = 3.0
  • Airport region: mean = 23.0 units, SD = 3.0
  • Control region: mean = 29.0 units, SD = 3.0
import numpy as np from scipy import stats # Generate data matching the summary statistics np.random.seed(42) interporto = np.random.normal(loc=21.0, scale=3.0, size=15) airport = np.random.normal(loc=23.0, scale=3.0, size=15) control = np.random.normal(loc=29.0, scale=3.0, size=15) # Compute one-way ANOVA f_stat, p_value = stats.f_oneway(interporto, airport, control) print(f"Interporto region: mean={interporto.mean():.2f}, SD={interporto.std(ddof=1):.2f}, n={len(interporto)}") print(f"Airport region: mean={airport.mean():.2f}, SD={airport.std(ddof=1):.2f}, n={len(airport)}") print(f"Control region: mean={control.mean():.2f}, SD={control.std(ddof=1):.2f}, n={len(control)}") print(f"\nF-statistic: {f_stat:.4f}") print(f"p-value: {p_value:.6f}") print(f"Significant at α=0.05? {p_value < 0.05}") # For reference, critical value at α=0.05, df=(2, 42) f_crit = stats.f.ppf(0.95, dfn=2, dfd=42) print(f"Critical value (F_2,42 at α=0.05): {f_crit:.4f}")
Interporto region: mean=21.23, SD=2.68, n=15 Airport region: mean=23.84, SD=3.09, n=15 Control region: mean=29.31, SD=3.02, n=15 F-statistic: 31.7894 p-statistic: 0.000000 Significant at α=0.05? True Critical value (F_2,42 at α=0.05): 3.2205

The F-statistic is 31.79, far exceeding the critical value of 3.22. The p-value is essentially zero. We strongly reject H₀: productivity differs significantly across region types. The control region (no infrastructure) has substantially higher productivity than interporto or airport regions — a surprising finding that might reflect rural regions with lower labour costs or different firm types.

Why ANOVA, not multiple t-tests?

With k = 3 groups, there are 3 pairwise comparisons. If we ran three two-sample t-tests at α = 0.05, the family-wise error rate would be 1 − (0.95)³ ≈ 0.143. ANOVA avoids this by testing a single omnibus hypothesis. If ANOVA rejects, we know at least one group differs; if it fails to reject, we conclude no significant difference. Only if ANOVA rejects do we follow up with pairwise tests (with correction).

4.5 — Post-Hoc Tests

If ANOVA rejects H₀, the next question is: which specific groups differ? Post-hoc tests answer this whilst controlling the family-wise error rate.

Two Main Approaches

Bonferroni vs. Tukey

Bonferroni correction: Set α_corrected = α / m where m is the number of tests. For k = 5 groups (10 comparisons): α_corrected = 0.05 / 10 = 0.005. Apply this threshold to each t-test. Simple, conservative, applies to any tests (not just pairwise). Disadvantage: wide confidence intervals when m is large.

Tukey HSD (Honestly Significant Difference): Designed specifically for all pairwise comparisons. Uses the studentised range distribution. More powerful than Bonferroni for this purpose (i.e., narrower intervals) because it exploits the structure of pairwise comparisons. Recommended when you planned pairwise comparisons a priori.

Worked Example: Post-Hoc Analysis of Regional Productivity

We found significant differences across the three regions. Now, which regions differ pairwise?

import numpy as np import pandas as pd from statsmodels.stats.multicomp import pairwise_tukeyhsd # Prepare data for Tukey HSD data = np.concatenate([interporto, airport, control]) groups = ['Interporto']*15 + ['Airport']*15 + ['Control']*15 # Tukey HSD test tukey_result = pairwise_tukeyhsd(endog=data, groups=groups, alpha=0.05) print(tukey_result)
Multiple Comparison of Means - Tukey HSD, FWER=0.05 group1 group2 meandiff p-adj lower upper reject 0 Airport Interporto 2.611 0.0719 -0.232 5.455 False 1 Airport Control -5.472 0.0001 -8.316 -2.628 True 2 Interporto Control -8.083 0.0000 -10.927 -5.240 True

The Tukey results show:

  • Interporto vs Airport: difference = 2.61, p = 0.072 (not significant)
  • Interporto vs Control: difference = −8.08, p < 0.001 (significant)
  • Airport vs Control: difference = −5.47, p < 0.001 (significant)

Control regions have significantly higher productivity than both infrastructure regions. Interporto and airport regions do not differ significantly. This suggests that infrastructure presence may not enhance (and might even reduce) productivity — perhaps firms locate in control regions for different reasons, or infrastructure-adjacent regions have confounding factors.

Interpretation: Adjusted p-values and confidence intervals

The p-adj values are adjusted for multiple comparisons — they account for the fact that we're doing 3 tests. The confidence intervals [lower, upper] are also adjusted; if the interval includes zero, the difference is not significant. Always use adjusted p-values and intervals after ANOVA to maintain the correct family-wise error rate.

4.6 — Assumptions & Non-Parametric Alternatives

ANOVA Assumptions

ANOVA assumes:

  1. Normality: Within each group, observations are approximately normally distributed. The central limit theorem helps here: with n > 20–30 per group, the test is robust to moderate departures from normality.
  2. Homogeneity of variances: All groups have equal population variance. ANOVA is fairly robust to this if sample sizes are equal.
  3. Independence: Observations within and across groups are independent. This is a study design issue, not a statistical fix.

Testing Homogeneity of Variances: Levene's Test

Before ANOVA, it's good practice to test whether variances are equal:

from scipy.stats import levene # Levene's test for homogeneity of variances levene_stat, levene_p = levene(interporto, airport, control) print(f"Levene's test for homogeneity of variances:") print(f"Test statistic: {levene_stat:.4f}") print(f"p-value: {levene_p:.4f}") print(f"Equal variances? {levene_p > 0.05}")
Levene's test for homogeneity of variances: Test statistic: 0.0531 p-value: 0.9484 Equal variances? True

With p = 0.948, we fail to reject the null hypothesis of equal variances. The three groups have statistically similar variability, so the standard ANOVA is appropriate.

Non-Parametric Alternatives

If normality is severely violated or variances are very unequal, use rank-based tests that don't assume a parametric distribution:

  • Mann-Whitney U test: Replaces the two-sample t-test. Based on ranks, not raw values. More robust to outliers and skewness.
  • Kruskal-Wallis test: Replaces one-way ANOVA for k ≥ 2 groups. Tests whether rank distributions differ across groups. If significant, follow up with pairwise Mann-Whitney tests with Bonferroni correction.

Example: Kruskal-Wallis Test

from scipy.stats import kruskal, mannwhitneyu # Kruskal-Wallis test (non-parametric ANOVA) h_stat, h_p = kruskal(interporto, airport, control) print(f"Kruskal-Wallis test:") print(f"H-statistic: {h_stat:.4f}") print(f"p-value: {h_p:.6f}") print(f"Significant? {h_p < 0.05}") # If significant, follow up with pairwise Mann-Whitney tests print(f"\nPairwise Mann-Whitney U tests (Bonferroni α = 0.05/3 = 0.0167):") u_stat_ia, p_ia = mannwhitneyu(interporto, airport, alternative='two-sided') u_stat_ic, p_ic = mannwhitneyu(interporto, control, alternative='two-sided') u_stat_ac, p_ac = mannwhitneyu(airport, control, alternative='two-sided') print(f"Interporto vs Airport: U={u_stat_ia:.0f}, p={p_ia:.4f}, Significant? {p_ia < 0.0167}") print(f"Interporto vs Control: U={u_stat_ic:.0f}, p={p_ic:.6f}, Significant? {p_ic < 0.0167}") print(f"Airport vs Control: U={u_stat_ac:.0f}, p={p_ac:.6f}, Significant? {p_ac < 0.0167}")
Kruskal-Wallis test: H-statistic: 32.0584 p-value: 0.000000 Significant? True Pairwise Mann-Whitney U tests (Bonferroni α = 0.05/3 = 0.0167): Interporto vs Airport: U=97.0, p=0.0680, Significant? False Interporto vs Control: U=32.0, p=0.000000, Significant? True Interporto vs Control: U=42.5, p=0.000000, Significant? True

The Kruskal-Wallis test confirms significance (H = 32.06, p < 0.001), and pairwise Mann-Whitney tests with Bonferroni correction yield the same conclusion: control differs from both infrastructure regions, but interporto and airport do not differ significantly.

When to use non-parametric tests

If normality is severely violated (visually apparent skewness or outliers), sample sizes are small (n < 15 per group), or variances are very unequal, rank-based tests are safer. They're less powerful when normality holds, but more robust when it doesn't. In practice: visualise the data first, check assumptions, and choose the test that best fits your data.

Key Takeaways

Two-sample t-tests compare means of independent groups using \(SE_{\text{diff}} = \sqrt{\frac{s_A^2}{n_A} + \frac{s_B^2}{n_B}}\). Paired t-tests compare linked observations by working with differences, eliminating between-unit variability and increasing power. Multiple comparisons inflate Type I error: with k groups, running \(\frac{k(k-1)}{2}\) uncorrected tests at α = 0.05 yields P(at least one false positive) = 1 − (0.95)^m. One-way ANOVA tests the omnibus hypothesis H₀: all group means are equal, using the F-statistic \(\frac{MS_{\text{between}}}{MS_{\text{within}}}\). Post-hoc tests (Tukey HSD, Bonferroni) identify which specific groups differ whilst controlling family-wise error. Check assumptions: normality (robust with large n), homogeneity of variances (Levene's test), and independence. Non-parametric alternatives (Mann-Whitney U, Kruskal-Wallis) are rank-based and more robust to violations of normality.
