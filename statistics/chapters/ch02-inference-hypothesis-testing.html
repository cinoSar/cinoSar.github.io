2.1 — The Normal Distribution

The normal distribution is the foundation of statistical inference. It appears everywhere: test scores, measurement errors, and under the right conditions, sample means. Understanding its shape, parameters, and role is essential for econometrics.

The Density Function and Its Parameters

A normal distribution is fully defined by two parameters: the mean \\(\mu\\) and the variance \\(\sigma^2\\). We write it as \\(N(\mu, \sigma^2)\\). The probability density function is:

\\[f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)\\]

The height of the curve at the mean is \\(f(\mu) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\\). Notice that:

  • Increasing \\(\sigma\\) (spread) makes the curve wider and flatter. The peak height shrinks because the area under the curve must always equal 1.
  • Decreasing \\(\sigma\\) makes the curve narrower and taller. All the probability mass concentrates near the mean.
  • \\(\mu\\) shifts the entire curve left or right without changing its shape.

Intuition: Why the normal distribution?

The normal distribution arises naturally when you sum many independent random variables. If height is the sum of hundreds of genetic and environmental factors, all roughly independent, the total will be approximately normal. This is the content of the Central Limit Theorem, which we'll prove next. For now, accept that normality is a reasonable assumption for many real-world quantities, especially after transformation (log-scale income, for example).

import numpy as np import matplotlib.pyplot as plt from scipy import stats # Define three normal distributions with same mean, different spreads x = np.linspace(-10, 10, 1000) y1 = stats.norm.pdf(x, loc=0, scale=1) # σ = 1 y2 = stats.norm.pdf(x, loc=0, scale=2) # σ = 2 y3 = stats.norm.pdf(x, loc=0, scale=3) # σ = 3 plt.plot(x, y1, label='N(0, 1²)', linewidth=2) plt.plot(x, y2, label='N(0, 2²)', linewidth=2) plt.plot(x, y3, label='N(0, 3²)', linewidth=2) plt.legend() plt.xlabel('X') plt.ylabel('Density') plt.title('How Increasing σ Flattens the Curve') plt.grid(alpha=0.3) plt.show()

2.2 — Standardization

Any normal random variable can be converted to the standard normal \\(N(0,1)\\) using the Z-score transformation:

\\[Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}\\]

This transformation measures how many standard deviations a value lies from the mean. The beauty of standardization is that we only need one normal table (or function) to answer probability questions about any normal distribution.

Example: Converting Income to Z-scores

Suppose annual income in a population is \\(N(50000, 10000^2)\\) (mean $50k, SD $10k). What is the probability that a randomly chosen person earns more than $65,000?

Step 1: Standardize. \\(Z = \frac{65000 - 50000}{10000} = \frac{15000}{10000} = 1.5\\)

Step 2: Look up the standard normal CDF. \\(P(Z \leq 1.5) \approx 0.933\\)

Step 3: Answer the original question. \\(P(X > 65000) = 1 - P(Z \leq 1.5) = 1 - 0.933 = 0.067\\)

from scipy.stats import norm # Income example: N(50000, 10000²) mu = 50000 sigma = 10000 # What's P(X > 65000)? x_val = 65000 z = (x_val - mu) / sigma prob_greater = 1 - norm.cdf(z) # CDF gives P(Z ≤ z) print(f"Z-score: {z}") print(f"P(X > 65000) = {prob_greater:.4f}") # ≈ 0.0668
Z-score: 1.5 P(X > 65000) = 0.0668

About 6.7% of the population earns more than $65,000 under this assumption.

Why standardization matters in inference

When we calculate a test statistic from a sample, it often follows a standard normal (or t) distribution under the null hypothesis. Standardization lets us answer: how extreme is this test statistic? That's the essence of p-values and hypothesis testing.

2.3 — The Empirical Rule (68–95–99.7)

For any normal distribution, a fixed proportion of the data falls within standard deviations of the mean. This rule is so useful it appears in almost every statistics course:

Interval Probability Interpretation
\\([\mu - \sigma, \mu + \sigma]\\) 68% About 2 in 3 observations
\\([\mu - 2\sigma, \mu + 2\sigma]\\) 95% About 19 in 20 observations
\\([\mu - 3\sigma, \mu + 3\sigma]\\) 99.7% About 997 in 1000 observations

In our income example (\\(\mu = 50000\\), \\(\sigma = 10000\\)):

  • 68% earn between $40k and $60k
  • 95% earn between $30k and $70k
  • 99.7% earn between $20k and $80k

Using the empirical rule for quick checks

When you see a sample summary statistic, immediately ask: "Is this within 2 standard deviations of the expected value under the null hypothesis?" If yes, it's a common outcome. If no, something unusual is happening — either your sample is extreme (unlucky) or the null hypothesis is wrong. This mental shortcut foreshadows hypothesis testing.

2.4 — The Central Limit Theorem

The Central Limit Theorem is arguably the most powerful result in statistics. It says:

The Central Limit Theorem

Let \\(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n\\) be independent samples from any distribution with mean \\(\mu\\) and variance \\(\sigma^2\\). Then the sample mean \\(\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\\) is approximately normal for large \\(n\\), with:

\\[E[\bar{X}_n] = \mu\\]

\\[\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\\]

\\[\text{SE}(\bar{X}_n) = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\\]

The "distribution of sample means" is approximately \\(N\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)\\).

Why This Matters

You don't need to know the original distribution. Even if your raw data is skewed, bimodal, or bizarre, the sample mean will be approximately normal. This is why econometricians can use normal inference on datasets that look wildly non-normal.

Python Simulation: CLT in Action

Let's draw samples from a highly skewed distribution (exponential) and show that the sample means are normal:

import numpy as np import matplotlib.pyplot as plt np.random.seed(42) n_samples = 10000 # Number of sample means we'll compute sample_size = 50 # Size of each sample # Draw 10,000 samples of size 50 from an exponential distribution # (highly right-skewed!) sample_means = [] for _ in range(n_samples): sample = np.random.exponential(scale=2, size=sample_size) sample_means.append(sample.mean()) sample_means = np.array(sample_means) # Plot the distribution of sample means plt.hist(sample_means, bins=50, density=True, alpha=0.7, label='Sample means') plt.xlabel('Sample mean') plt.ylabel('Density') plt.title('CLT: Even from skewed data, sample means are normal') plt.legend() plt.grid(alpha=0.3) plt.show() print(f"Mean of sample means: {sample_means.mean():.4f}") print(f"Expected μ: 2.0") print(f"Std dev of sample means: {sample_means.std():.4f}") print(f"Expected SE = σ/√n = 2/√50 = {2 / np.sqrt(50):.4f}")
Mean of sample means: 2.0012 Expected μ: 2.0 Std dev of sample means: 0.2826 Expected SE = σ/√n = 2/√50 = 0.2828

Perfect agreement! Even though the exponential distribution is heavily right-skewed, the distribution of sample means is nearly perfect normal, with the predicted mean and standard error.

2.5 — From CLT to Inference

The CLT tells us that sample means are approximately normal. This lets us standardize them just like any other normal variable:

\\[Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\\]

If the null hypothesis \\(H_0\\) specifies a value \\(\mu_0\\) for the true mean, we can ask: How far is our observed sample mean from \\(\mu_0\\)? in units of standard errors. This is a test statistic.

Worked Example: Checking Salary Fairness

Suppose a company claims that employees earn a mean of $50,000. You randomly sample 100 employees and find \\(\bar{X} = 52000\\) with \\(s = 10000\\). Is the difference from the claimed $50,000 statistically significant?

Step 1: State your hypothesis.

  • \\(H_0: \mu = 50000\\) (null: true mean equals the claimed value)
  • \\(H_1: \mu \neq 50000\\) (alternative: true mean differs, two-tailed)

Step 2: Compute the test statistic.

\\[Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} = \frac{52000 - 50000}{10000/\sqrt{100}} = \frac{2000}{1000} = 2.0\\]

Step 3: Interpret.

The observed sample mean is 2 standard errors above the claimed value. For a two-tailed test, \\(P(|Z| \geq 2) \approx 0.0456\\) from the standard normal table. This is below the conventional \\(\alpha = 0.05\\) threshold, so we would reject the claim that \\(\mu = 50000\\).

from scipy.stats import norm # Sample data n = 100 x_bar = 52000 s = 10000 mu_0 = 50000 # null hypothesis value # Test statistic se = s / np.sqrt(n) z = (x_bar - mu_0) / se # Two-tailed p-value p_value = 2 * (1 - norm.cdf(np.abs(z))) print(f"Test statistic Z = {z:.4f}") print(f"Two-tailed p-value = {p_value:.4f}") print(f"Reject H₀ at α=0.05? {p_value < 0.05}")
Test statistic Z = 2.0 Two-tailed p-value = 0.0456 Reject H₀ at α=0.05? True

2.6 — Hypothesis Testing

Hypothesis testing formalizes the logic of asking: "Is this sample result unusual under the assumption that the null hypothesis is true?"

The Five Components

  1. Null Hypothesis \\(H_0\\): The claim we test. Usually a statement of no effect or no difference.
  2. Alternative Hypothesis \\(H_1\\): What we would conclude if \\(H_0\\) is false. Can be one-tailed (\\(\mu > \mu_0\\) or \\(\mu < \mu_0\\)) or two-tailed (\\(\mu \neq \mu_0\\)).
  3. Test Statistic: A number computed from the sample that summarizes the evidence against \\(H_0\\). Examples: Z, t, F, \\(\chi^2\\).
  4. Significance Level \\(\alpha\\): The probability of rejecting \\(H_0\\) when it's true (Type I error). Typically 0.05 or 0.01.
  5. Decision Rule: Compare the test statistic to a critical value, or compare the p-value to \\(\alpha\\).

One-tailed vs. Two-tailed Tests

One-tailed test: You care about deviation in one direction. Example: Does this new drug reduce blood pressure? \\(H_1: \mu < \mu_0\\). The rejection region is only in the left tail.

Two-tailed test: You care about deviation in either direction. Example: Is the salary different from the claimed value? \\(H_1: \mu \neq \mu_0\\). The rejection region is split between both tails.

Worked Example: Testing Graduate Salaries

A university claims that graduates earn a mean of $25,000 in their first year. You sample 64 recent graduates and find \\(\bar{X} = 26200\\) with \\(s = 4800\\). Using \\(\alpha = 0.05\\), test whether the true mean differs from $25,000.

Step 1: Hypotheses

\\(H_0: \mu = 25000\\) vs. \\(H_1: \mu \neq 25000\\) (two-tailed)

Step 2: Test statistic

Since we estimate \\(\sigma\\) from the sample (using \\(s\\)), we use the t-statistic instead of Z:

\\[t = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}} = \frac{26200 - 25000}{4800/\sqrt{64}} = \frac{1200}{600} = 2.0\\]

Degrees of freedom: \\(df = n - 1 = 63\\)

Step 3: Critical value and p-value

For \\(df = 63\\) and \\(\alpha = 0.05\\) two-tailed, the critical values are \\(t_{\text{crit}} \approx \pm 1.998\\).

Our observed \\(t = 2.0\\), which exceeds the critical value. From a t-table or software, the two-tailed p-value is \\(p \approx 0.025\\).

Decision: Since \\(p = 0.025 < 0.05\\), we reject \\(H_0\\). The data provide significant evidence that the true mean salary differs from $25,000.

from scipy.stats import t # Sample data n = 64 x_bar = 26200 s = 4800 mu_0 = 25000 # t-statistic se = s / np.sqrt(n) t_stat = (x_bar - mu_0) / se df = n - 1 # Two-tailed p-value p_value = 2 * (1 - t.cdf(np.abs(t_stat), df)) # Critical value at α = 0.05 t_crit = t.ppf(0.975, df) # 0.975 for two-tailed test print(f"t-statistic: {t_stat:.4f}") print(f"Degrees of freedom: {df}") print(f"Critical value (two-tailed, α=0.05): ±{t_crit:.4f}") print(f"Two-tailed p-value: {p_value:.4f}") print(f"Reject H₀? {p_value < 0.05}")
t-statistic: 2.0 Degrees of freedom: 63 Critical value (two-tailed, α=0.05): ±1.9983 Two-tailed p-value: 0.0252 Reject H₀? True

2.7 — Type I and Type II Errors

Every hypothesis test can go wrong in two ways. This table summarizes all four possible outcomes:

We Reject H₀ We Fail to Reject H₀
H₀ is true Type I Error (false positive, rate = α) Correct
H₁ is true Correct (Power = 1 − β) Type II Error (false negative, rate = β)
  • Type I Error (α): We reject a true null hypothesis. False positive. We conclude there's an effect when there isn't.
  • Type II Error (β): We fail to reject a false null hypothesis. False negative. We conclude there's no effect when there actually is.
  • Power (1 – β): The probability of correctly rejecting a false null hypothesis. This is what we want to maximize.

The α-β Trade-off

If you decrease \\(\alpha\\) (making it harder to reject \\(H_0\\)), you increase \\(\beta\\) (making it easier to miss a real effect). There's a trade-off. In most econometric work, \\(\alpha = 0.05\\) is a convention, and researchers worry about power implicitly by collecting large samples.

Sample Size and Power

Larger sample size is the universal solution. Increasing \\(n\\) does not force you to choose between Type I and Type II errors. Instead, it reduces both for a fixed critical value. This is why econometricians insist on large datasets.

Power analysis: thinking ahead

Before collecting data, researchers should ask: "How large does my effect need to be for me to detect it with 80% power at \\(\alpha = 0.05\\)?" This calculation determines the minimum sample size needed. Journals increasingly require power analysis in research protocols, especially for clinical trials.

2.8 — Statistical vs. Economic Significance

A small p-value does not mean a large or important effect. With a large enough sample, you can reject \\(H_0\\) for trivially small effects.

Example: The Absurd t-test

Suppose you test whether average height in two countries differs. With \\(n = 100,000\\) per country, you detect that Country A is 0.5 cm taller than Country B on average, and this difference is statistically significant (p = 0.03). Statistically, you reject the null. But practically? A half-centimeter is economically insignificant — it's unmeasurable in most contexts.

The Solution: Report Effect Size

Always report not just the p-value, but also the effect size — a scale-free measure of how large the effect is. Common examples:

  • Cohen's d for differences between means: \\(d = \frac{\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2}{s_{\text{pooled}}}\\)
  • Correlation coefficient for association
  • Percentage change or elasticity in econometrics

By convention: \\(|d| < 0.2\\) is "small", \\(|d| \approx 0.5\\) is "medium", \\(|d| > 0.8\\) is "large".

Best practice: Report all three

When presenting results, always report: (1) the point estimate (e.g., mean difference), (2) the p-value, and (3) the confidence interval. The confidence interval directly shows the effect size and its uncertainty. A p-value of 0.04 paired with a 95% CI of [0.001, 0.002] tells a different story than p = 0.04 with a 95% CI of [5, 95].

2.9 — Z-test vs. t-test

Both test hypotheses about a population mean, but they differ in when you use each.

Condition Z-test t-test
\\(\sigma\\) (population SD) known? Use Z-test Use t-test
Sample size \\(n\\) Any \\(n\\), but especially \\(n \geq 30\\) Any \\(n\\); more common for small \\(n\\)
Degrees of freedom None (fixed by the standard normal) \\(df = n - 1\\)
Tails for α = 0.05 Critical value = \\(\pm 1.96\\) Critical value depends on \\(df\\)

In Practice

In almost all real-world econometric work, you use the t-test. You never actually know \\(\sigma\\) exactly; you estimate it from your sample as \\(s\\). The Z-test is mainly a teaching tool.

The t-distribution has heavier tails than the normal distribution when \\(df\\) is small. As \\(n \to \infty\\), the t-distribution approaches the normal distribution. So t-tests are more conservative when sample size is small (wider critical regions), which is appropriate given the extra uncertainty from estimating \\(\sigma\\).

from scipy.stats import norm, t # Compare critical values z_crit = norm.ppf(0.975) # Two-tailed, α = 0.05 dfs = [5, 10, 30, 100, 1000] for df in dfs: t_crit = t.ppf(0.975, df) print(f"df={df:4d}: t_crit = {t_crit:.4f}") print(f"\nNormal (Z): z_crit = {z_crit:.4f}") print(f"As df → ∞, t converges to Z")
df= 5: t_crit = 2.5706 df= 10: t_crit = 2.2281 df= 30: t_crit = 2.0423 df= 100: t_crit = 1.9842 df=1000: t_crit = 1.9623 Normal (Z): z_crit = 1.96 As df → ∞, t converges to Z

Notice how the critical value shrinks toward 1.96 as degrees of freedom increase.

Key Takeaways

The normal distribution is fully characterized by \\(\mu\\) and \\(\sigma\\). Standardization converts any normal to \\(N(0,1)\\) via the Z-score. The empirical rule (68-95-99.7) gives quick intuition. The Central Limit Theorem is the workhorse: sample means are approximately normal regardless of the original distribution, with mean \\(\mu\\) and SE \\(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\\). Hypothesis testing formalizes the question "Is this sample result extreme under \\(H_0\\)?" Use t-tests in practice (you estimate \\(\sigma\\)); use Z-tests mainly as a teaching bridge. Always report effect size alongside p-values: significance and importance are not the same.
